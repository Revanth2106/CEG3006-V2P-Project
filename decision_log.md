| 3 | 10/03 | Need to select V2P communication technology | (a) DSRC / IEEE 802.11p<br>(b) C-V2X<br>(c) LTE / Bluetooth | Latency, range, power consumption | DSRC / IEEE 802.11p selected. Operates on the 5.9 GHz band, offers ~100 ms latency, range up to 100m to 300m, and requires no cellular infrastructure. C-V2X rejected as it requires newer hardware with limited pedestrian device support; BLE alone insufficient for vehicle closing speeds. | ChatGPT provided initial comparison; team verified latency and range figures independently | Revanth, Peng Hui |
| 4 | 12/03 | DSRC radio module cannot be embedded in insole — too large and power-hungry | (a) Embed full DSRC module in insole<br>(b) Use paired smartphone as relay | Power budget, form factor, user convenience | Smartphone as relay adopted. Adds BLE relay latency but avoids bulky insole hardware. | Gemini AI suggested the relay architecture; team confirmed BLE relay latency values using published BLE specs | Revanth, Ernest |
| 5 | 15/03 | Need to define vibration pattern language (what each buzz means) | (a) Intensity only (stronger = more urgent)<br>(b) Spatial only (which foot)<br>(c) Combined spatial + pattern coding | Learnability, reaction time, suitability for visually impaired users | Combined spatial + pattern adopted. Left/right foot indicates danger direction; pulse pattern encodes urgency (single pulse = caution, double rapid pulse = imminent). Aligns with research on tactile pattern learnability; avoids information overload. | Gemini AI drafted initial pattern options; team consulted a paper on haptic icon design to validate learnability claim | Peng Hui, Yong Lun |
| 6 | 18/03 | Need to determine minimum safe warning time (latency budget) | Derived from vehicle kinematics: at 50 km/h a car covers ~14 m/s; pedestrian needs ~1.5 s to react and step back | End-to-end latency must be ≤ 500 ms to allow margin within the ~1.5 s reaction window | 500 ms end-to-end latency budget set. Breakdown: 802.11p transmission ~50 ms, smartphone processing ~50 ms, BLE relay ~10 ms, app logic ~20 ms. Total ~130 ms, well within budget. Provides margin for channel congestion at busy intersections. | Used Claude to draft latency budget; team verified 802.11p figures against IEEE 802.11p standard | Peng Hui, Revanth |
| 7 | 21/03 | Privacy concern raised: continuous GPS broadcasting by pedestrian's phone could expose location | (a) Phone broadcasts position continuously<br>(b) Phone only listens, vehicle broadcasts | User privacy, regulatory compliance, implementation complexity | Pedestrian device listens only (infrastructure push model). Vehicles and RSUs broadcast BSMs/CAMs; the insole app receives and processes these locally. No pedestrian location is ever transmitted. | Gemini AI flagged privacy issue unprompted — team had not previously considered it; human decision to adopt listen-only model | Wiz, Ernest |
| 8 | 23/03 | Need to select insole actuator type | (a) Eccentric rotating mass (ERM) motors<br>(b) Linear resonant actuators (LRA)<br>(c) Piezoelectric actuators | Response time, power draw, size, durability under foot pressure | LRA actuators selected (four per insole, namely Left, Right, Center, Global). Faster response (~10 ms vs ~50 ms for ERM), more energy efficient, and better suited to repeatable pulse patterns. Piezoelectric rejected due to high driving voltage incompatible with a small battery. | Claude provided actuator comparison; team verified response time specs from TI application note on LRAs | Wiz, Yong Lun |
| 9 | 24/03 | Battery life concern — insole must last a full day | (a) Larger LiPo battery (heavier)<br>(b) Aggressive duty cycling of BLE<br>(c) Energy harvesting from walking | Battery life target ≥ 8 hours, added weight ≤ 50 g per insole, cost | Duty-cycled BLE + 200 mAh LiPo selected. BLE connection interval tuned to 100 ms (acceptable added latency). Energy harvesting rejected as insufficient and mechanically complex for insole form factor. | Claude estimated power draw; team validated against Nordic nRF52 datasheet figures | Ernest, Peng Hui |
| 10 | 25/03 | False positive alerts identified in dense urban areas with many parked and distant vehicles | (a) Increase distance threshold<br>(b) Add heading/velocity filter — only alert if vehicle is moving toward pedestrian<br>(c) Use RSU whitelist to suppress parked vehicle beacons | False positive rate, true positive rate, implementation complexity | A two-layer filtering approach was implemented in app logic. First, a distance threshold of 150 m ensures only nearby vehicles are processed — derived from kinematics: at 50 km/h a vehicle covers 150 m in ~10.8 s, providing sufficient warning time while reducing unnecessary processing load. Second, a heading and velocity filter discards BSM messages from vehicles with speed < 5 km/h (considered stationary) or heading diverging > 45° from the pedestrian path — determined through geometric analysis, as any vehicle heading more than 45° away is physically moving out of the crossing zone. Since DSRC broadcasts BSMs at 10 Hz, the filter reassesses every 100 ms allowing rapid dismissal of non-threatening vehicles. | Claude suggested the initial filter concept; threshold values were derived independently by the team through kinematic and geometric analysis | Ernest, Wiz |
